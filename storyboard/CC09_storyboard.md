## Slide 07 — C P R in Action

Slide-ID: 1S07
Template-ID: content-split
Slide-Title: C P R in Action — The Brake Service Objection
Caption-Text: Here is C P R applied to a real, vague brake-service pushback.
On-Screen-Text: Customer: "I am just not sure I really need those brakes done today." Technician (Clarify): "When you say you are not sure — is it the cost, the timing, or are you not convinced the service is necessary?" Customer: "Well, the car seems to be stopping fine to me." Technician (Paraphrase): "So it sounds like you are questioning whether the service is needed right now, since you have not noticed a problem." Customer: "Exactly." Technician (Respond): "That makes sense. Let me show you what I found. Your pads are at 2 millimeters — that is Porsha's minimum specification. You have not noticed it yet because the wear has been gradual, but you are very close to metal-on-metal, which would damage your rotors and cost significantly more to repair."
Callout-Text: The Respond step works because Clarify and Paraphrase already told you what to respond to.
Callout-Label: Why It Works
Image-File: 1S07.webp
Image: Close-up of a worn Porsha brake pad held in a gloved hand next to a measurement tool or a new pad for comparison. Clear visual evidence — the kind of thing a technician would show a customer during the Respond step.
Voiceover-INTRO: Watch what is happening here. The customer's first sentence is vague — "I am not sure I really need those brakes." A weak response would be to start defending the recommendation, listing reasons. Instead, the technician Clarifies with a multiple-choice question. The customer's answer — "the car seems to be stopping fine" — tells you the real concern is perceived necessity, not cost or timing. The Paraphrase locks that in. And only then does the Respond happen — with physical evidence and a clear consequence. You did not guess. You did not over-explain. You answered the actual question.

## Slide 10 — The R A C E Framework

Slide-ID: 1S08
Template-ID: card-explore
Slide-Title: The R A C E Framework — When a Customer Mentions a Competitor
Caption-Text: R A C E lets you address competitor comparisons without going negative.
On-Screen-Text: When a customer brings up an independent shop or a competitor by name, R A C E gives you a structure that respects their thinking and earns the right to make the Porsha case. Click each card to explore.
Image-File: 1S08.webp
Image: A four-step horizontal flow graphic — handshake icon, two parallel arrows merging, a divider with "important differences" text, and an upward arrow. Clean visual hierarchy, Porsha brand styling.
Voiceover-INTRO: R A C E — Respect, Align, Contrast, Elevate — is the four-step move for customers who see Porsha service and the shop down the street as essentially equivalent. The order matters. Skipping a step makes the whole thing feel like a sales pitch. Click each card to explore.
Voiceover-CLICK-Respect: Respect means saying something genuinely positive about the competitor. Not sarcastic — actually positive. "Franz's has been around a long time" or "That's a reputable shop." This is where most technicians want to skip ahead. Don't. Skipping Respect signals defensiveness, and the customer will read it. Done right, it tells the customer you are not threatened — and that earns you the right to keep talking.
Card-Title-Respect: Respect
Card-Sig-Respect: Acknowledge
Card-Bullets-Respect: Say something positive about the competitor | Never badmouth or criticize | Sets a non-defensive tone
Voiceover-CLICK-Align: Align means listing what Porsha service and the competitor have in common. "They have experienced technicians. They can do basic service. In a lot of ways, yes — the service is similar." You are not conceding anything. You are showing the customer you can see things clearly, which makes everything you say next more credible.
Card-Title-Align: Align
Card-Sig-Align: Find Common Ground
Card-Bullets-Align: List genuine similarities | Be fair and objective | Build credibility for what comes next
Voiceover-CLICK-Contrast: Contrast uses one specific phrase — "There are some important differences." Not "but." Not "however." Those two words erase everything you just said. The customer hears "I just lied about them being good." The exact phrase keeps the tone collaborative — you are offering more information, not arguing.
Card-Title-Contrast: Contrast
Card-Sig-Contrast: Pivot
Card-Bullets-Contrast: Use the exact phrase "There are some important differences" | Never use "but" or "however" | Keeps the tone informative, not adversarial
Voiceover-CLICK-Elevate: Elevate is where you describe what only Porsha service offers — Porsha Genuine Parts with warranty coverage, factory-certified technicians with annual training hours, Porsha-specific diagnostic equipment, access to technical bulletins. Concrete advantages, not slogans. You earned the right to say this because you respected the competitor, aligned on what is the same, and contrasted without erasing.
Card-Title-Elevate: Elevate
Card-Sig-Elevate: Differentiate
Card-Bullets-Elevate: Name what only Porsha can offer | Use concrete advantages, not slogans | Connect features to the customer's vehicle

## Slide 16 — Professionalism Under Pressure

Slide-ID: 1S12
Template-ID: tab-panel
Slide-Title: Professionalism Under Pressure
Caption-Text: Four principles for staying professional when a customer pushes back.
On-Screen-Text: Frameworks help you respond. Professionalism is how you carry yourself while you do it. Select each tab to explore.
Image-File: 1S12.webp
Image: A Porsha technician in service uniform, calm posture, listening to a customer at a write-up counter. Neutral expression, confident body language. The customer is gesturing — the technician is steady. Service center interior, warm light.
Voiceover-INTRO: How you handle an objection matters more than the framework you choose. Four principles keep the conversation professional even when the customer is frustrated or skeptical. Select each tab to explore.
Voiceover-TAB-StayCalm: Stay calm and confident. Questions are not attacks. The customer is doing what informed customers do — they are checking your work, weighing the cost, and making sure the recommendation makes sense. Your tone should stay helpful, not defensive. Confidence comes from knowing your expertise, not from raising your voice or speaking faster.
Tab-Body-StayCalm: Treat questions as normal customer behavior, not personal attacks. Steady tone, helpful posture, no defensiveness.
Voiceover-TAB-NoBadmouthing: Never speak poorly of the competition. Even when a customer brings up another shop by name, even when you think they are wrong — badmouthing makes you look insecure. Customers respect professionals who can describe their own value without tearing down anyone else's. Focus on what you do, not what others do not.
Tab-Body-NoBadmouthing: Never criticize competitors, even when the customer invites it. Talk about your value, not their flaws. Even if you find issues with another shop's work, focus on what needs to be done now — not on assigning blame.
Voiceover-TAB-NoScareTactics: Be honest about urgency. There is a real difference between "this needs to be done in the next thousand miles" and "this needs to be done today." Overstate it and you might win the sale this time — and lose the customer forever the moment they catch on. Trust gets built on accurate severity, not manufactured crisis.
Tab-Body-NoScareTactics: Describe urgency accurately. Do not exaggerate to pressure the customer. Customers remember when you overstated something.
Voiceover-TAB-CustomerInterest: Stay focused on the customer's best interest. You are a technical advisor, not a closer. Sometimes the right answer is "you can safely wait six months on this." Saying that builds more long-term value than pushing every recommendation through. Customers come back to people who tell them the truth.
Tab-Body-CustomerInterest: You are a technical advisor, not a salesperson. Honest recommendations — including "you can wait" — earn long-term loyalty.
